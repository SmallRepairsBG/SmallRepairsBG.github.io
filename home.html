About the Vehicle Routing Problem (VRP) with Preferences

The class of VRPs contains a number of combinatorial optimisation and integer programming problems seeking to service a number of requests with a fleet of vehicles. Since the first VRP formulation, proposed by Dantzig and Ramser in 1959, many variants of the original problem have been proved useful in the areas of transportation, distribution, and logistics. Formally, each of them can be decomposed as follows:

VRP Variant := VRP Model + VRP Objective.

Most existing academic VRP instances and many practical VRP instances come in different formats. This makes the comparison of solution methods cumbersome because such methods take different instance format and produce different solution format. Hundreds of survey papers try to compensate for this by discussing how algorithms perform in various experimental settings. However, as such settings are designed under different assumptions, most survey results offer merely fair comparison of solution methods. becase ignore the multi-facete preferences drivers and customers might have for services. About the VRP PrefLib repository

VRP PrefLib is a reference library of benchmarks that mix vehicle data, request data, and preference data. Vehicle data models vehicles' available capacities, operation shifts and costs, gas emissions, and safety levels. Request data represents a number of customer locations for service tasks and times. Preference data models eligibility between drivers and customers, driver (profit) rankings over customers, customer (cost) rankings over drivers, multiple shift windows for drivers, and multiple time windows for customers.

The library is maintained by Martin Aleksandrov. It aims to provides a comprehensive resource of social vehicle routing problem instances and tools for them, that could be useful for multiple communities that deal with fleet management under preferences: vehicle routing and scheduling (combinatorial optimization), fair division and stability (computational social choice), policy making and integration (constraint satisfaction), as well as sensing and monitoring (geographic information systems).

In particular, the library provides carefully curated data, formatted in a unified format. Users might wish to read the descriptions of our models and check out the relations between them, before they explore our datasets, and our tools for generating instances, as well as our tools for visualizing instances and solutions to them. Lastly, users could learn more about the library by consulting our list of collected publications that use data from it.
